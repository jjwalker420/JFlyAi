## What this is, in one page

Here is the business in one sentence.

**You help busy restaurant groups turn what lives in their best people's heads into clear written playbooks, so every location runs the same way and every new opening starts from a proven plan instead of a blank page.**

That is it. You are not cooking. You are not running a floor at night. You are not on a clock. You are writing down how good operations actually work, for the people who need it written down and do not have time to do it themselves.

Here is what your day looks like.

You sit down at home with a cup of coffee. You pick one document to work on, say a server training manual. You open Claude on your computer and you talk to it for about ten minutes, the same way you would explain the job to a sharp new manager. Claude writes up a clean first draft while you talk. You read it, fix anything that is not quite how you do it, and save it. You are done for the day if you want to be. The next day you pick the next document.

That is the whole rhythm. Talk, review, save. You already know everything you need to know. The tool just helps you get it onto the page fast.

You do this from your couch, your kitchen table, or your bed on a rough day. There is no commute, no shift, no boss standing over you. You decide when you work and how much.

## The at home playbook, step by step

This section is the how to. Follow it in order the first time. After that you will not need it.

### Step 1. Set up Claude

Claude is the assistant you will talk to. It types up your knowledge while you explain it.

1. Go to claude.ai and create an account with your email.
2. Subscribe to the plan called **Claude Pro**. It costs about 20 dollars a month. That is your only required cost to run this entire business. No office, no software suite, nothing else.
3. Pro includes the **desktop app**, sometimes called Cowork. Download it to your computer. The desktop app is the one you want, because it lets you talk through a task and watch Claude write it up, and it can work right alongside your files.
4. Open the app and sign in. You will see a simple box where you type or talk. That is all there is to it.

That is your whole setup. Twenty dollars a month and an afternoon.

### Step 2. Organize your folders

Keep it simple. On your computer, make one main folder called **My Documents Business**. Inside it, make these folders.

- **Clients.** One folder per group you work with. Start with one called Culinary Creative.
- **Finished.** Every completed document goes here.
- **Templates.** Your reusable versions, with the client's name taken out, so you can sell the same document to the next group later.
- **Samples.** Your proof pieces you show before anyone hires you.

When you finish a document, save it in Finished, then save a clean copy in Templates with the names removed. That second copy is money in the bank for later. We will explain why in the section on where this can grow.

### Step 3. Make one document, start to finish

This is the heart of the whole business. Once you can do this, you can do everything. Here is the exact walkthrough. We will use a server training manual as the example.

**Talk for about ten minutes.** Open the Claude desktop app. Tell it what you want in plain words. You can type it or say it out loud. Something like this.

> I spent 25 years running restaurants and bars. I am writing a server training manual for a casual neighborhood burger and beer spot. I am going to talk you through how a server really learns this job, start to shift. Take notes and ask me questions if I skip something. Then write it up as a clean training manual a brand new server could follow.

Then just talk, the way you would walk a new manager through it. How does a server's shift start. What do they set up. What are the steps of service from greeting to goodbye. How do they handle a complaint. How do they close out. Do not worry about order or grammar. Just get it out of your head. If you forget something, Claude will ask. That is what it is there for.

**Let Claude write it up.** When you are done talking, say "okay, write the full manual now." Claude will turn your ten minutes of talking into a clean, organized document with headings, steps, and sections. This takes seconds.

**Read it and fix anything wrong.** This is your most important job, and only you can do it. Read the draft. Where it got something wrong, or made it too fancy, or missed how you actually do it, tell Claude. Say "the closing section is wrong, here is how we really close" and talk again. It fixes it instantly. Repeat until it sounds like you. Usually two or three rounds.

**Save it.** When it is right, copy the document into your Finished folder. Give it a clear name like "Server Training Manual, Tap and Burger." Save a clean copy in Templates with the client name removed. Done.

That is one finished, sellable document. Start to finish, maybe an hour the first time, faster after that. You did the thinking. The tool did the typing.

There are ready made prompts for every document type in the file called copy-paste-prompts.md. You can paste one in, fill in a couple of blanks, and start talking. That file is your shortcut so you never face a blank screen.

## Pricing, grounded in real numbers

Every number here sits inside what real restaurant and operations consultants charge in 2026. The research notes file shows the sources. There are three ways to charge. The retainer is the one I recommend, and I will explain why.

### The anchor, so you know your value
If this group hired an outside consulting firm to build their whole operations and opening system from the outside, it would realistically cost them **30,000 to 75,000 dollars or more**, plus travel, plus weeks of that firm learning their concepts from scratch. You already know their floors. Your discovery time is close to zero. That is your edge, and it is why your prices are fair and still a bargain for them.

### Way 1. Per document
Charge for each finished document. Good for testing the water or for one off needs.
- Simple documents, like a checklist or a one page procedure: **150 to 300 dollars** each.
- Standard documents, like a full role training manual or a multi step procedure: **400 to 750 dollars** each.
- The new location opening playbook, sold as its own package: **3,500 to 6,000 dollars**.

### Way 2. Monthly retainer, recommended
You agree to build a set amount of work each month for a flat monthly fee. This is the best model for you, and here is why. It gives you steady, predictable income. It does not punish you on a slow week or a hard health day, because they are paying for the finished work over the month, not for your hours. And it matches how a growing group actually needs you, a little at a time, ongoing.

- **Build period: 3,000 dollars per month.** This is while you are building the core system, usually three to six months. Most groups land somewhere between 9,000 and 18,000 dollars total for the full core build, depending on pace.
- **Maintenance: 1,000 dollars per month** after the build. This keeps everything current as they open new spots, change menus, and adjust procedures. It is light work for you and steady money, and it keeps you as their person.

### Way 3. Project fee
One fixed price for a defined, complete system, paid in milestones as you finish each part.
- **Full core operations and training system for one concept: 18,000 to 25,000 dollars.**
- Paid in thirds. One third to start, one third at the halfway mark, one third on delivery.

Use the project fee when a client wants a fixed, complete scope delivered by a certain date. Use per document to start small. Steer toward the retainer whenever you can, because steady beats lumpy, especially for you.

First, the most important rule. **Stay in your lane and say so out loud.** The group already has a Director of Human Resources and a recruiting manager. When you make that clear, you are not a threat to anyone. You are a missing piece that makes the HR team's job easier, because well trained people on documented systems are easier to manage.

Second, an angle that is working in your favor right now. The group's new Chief Executive, Richard Flaherty, came from Punch Bowl Social and Wagamama. Those are brands built on repeatable systems and openings that run the same way every time. A leader from that world already believes in written playbooks. You will not have to convince him that systems matter. You only have to show him you can build them for these concepts, from the inside, fast. One quick note. Leadership there changed recently, so before you walk in, confirm the current names and titles of whoever you are talking to. It is a small thing that keeps you looking sharp.

Third, name the payoff in their words. A systems minded leader buys results, not paper. So say what the work is worth. A documented opening playbook can save weeks of work on every new location and keep the guest experience the same across all of them. That is real money and real reputation. Put it in plain numbers if you can, like the days saved on the next opening.

Here is your script. It is short and humble. Pick the version that fits where you are.

### Version A, if you are still on salary there
Use this with whoever you report to, in a calm one on one.

> Can I run an idea by you. We keep opening new spots, and every time, a lot of the training and the procedures get rebuilt from scratch, because so much of how we run is in people's heads. I have spent my whole career building exactly that kind of thing, training manuals, opening playbooks, the operating systems. I would like to start writing ours down, location by location, so every spot runs the same and every opening starts from a proven plan. I can do this on the side, on my own time, without stepping on HR or recruiting at all. This is pure floor operations. Could we try one document, my treat, and you tell me if it is useful.

That last line is the key. You offer one free sample. Almost no one says no to free and useful.

### Version B, if you are fully off work right now
Use this as a friendly outreach to your former manager or the operations leader.

> I have been building something I think the group could really use. As we keep opening locations, the training and the opening procedures get rebuilt every time, and a lot of it lives in people's heads. I have put together a way to capture all of that into clean playbooks, location by location, so openings get easier and every spot runs consistent. In both versions you lead with the problem they already feel, you point to your track record, you make the HR boundary clear, and you ask for one small yes. That is the whole pitch.

### If you are still on salary, set it up the right way

This is the one part to handle carefully, so it never becomes awkward. If you pitch your own employer while you are still on their payroll, the fair question they may think is, "is this not just part of his job already." Get ahead of it, calmly and in the open.

- **Get a yes in writing before you do any paid work.** A short, friendly email is enough. Confirm that you will build these documents as a separate paid arrangement, outside your regular duties, and that they are happy with that. This protects both of you.
- **Be clear about what belongs to whom.** Work you do as part of your salaried job usually belongs to the company. This paid arrangement is for work beyond your role. Your reusable templates, with the names and private details removed, stay yours. Say that plainly and put it in writing too. There is a simple plain language scope note in this folder, the file named Simple-Agreement-and-Scope.md, that you can use for all of this.
- **If they say "just do this as part of your job," you still win.** You have two good moves. One, agree on a defined extra project with its own scope and a stipend or bonus, so the extra work is recognized and paid. Two, keep it warm and revisit a paid arrangement when you move off salary, while you keep building your own library in the meantime. Either way, you proved the value, and that is the real goal.

### How to talk about how you work

You will wonder whether to mention the tool. Here is the simple truth that keeps you honest and keeps your value clear.

- **You are selling 25 years of knowing what actually works on a floor.** That is the product. The tool is just how you type it up fast.
